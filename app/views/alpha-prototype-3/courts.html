Courts

You can arrange child maintenance using a consent order (in England and Wales), or a minute of agreement (in Scotland). This makes the arrangement legally binding. It can be enforced through the courts if either parent does not do what has been agreed.

You cannot apply to the Child Maintenance Service for a statutory child maintenance arrangement within 12 months of setting up a consent order or a minute of agreement. If you have a consent order or minute of agreement made before April 2003, you cannot set up a statutory arrangement.

Other ways to make child arrangements

You can get information about how to prepare to make child arrangements, and how to reach an agreement, from the get help with child arrangements service.

Change or enforce an order

If the other parent is not following the court order you have in place, you can ask the court to enforce it.

You cannot apply to the Child Maintenance Service for a statutory child maintenance arrangement within 12 months of setting up a consent order or minute of agreement. If you have a consent order or minute of agreement made before April 2003, you cannot set up a statutory arrangement.

Arranging child maintenance yourself

You can decide to do something different from the court order, if you both agree to the change. This is called a ‘family-based arrangement’ .
