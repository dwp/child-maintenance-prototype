What is an arrangement?

Using the Child Maintenance Service is not compulsory and you can make an arrangement between yourselves.

Arranging child maintenance between yourselves

There are many ways to arrange child maintenance between yourselves.

For example, you could agree to share care of the child, or that the non-resident parent will pay a proportion of their income to the parent with care.

Or the non-resident parent could pay:

  • for things like housing costs, uniforms, clothes, school trips, or after school clubs
  • larger lump sums at certain times in the child’s life

If you choose to make your own financial arrangement, both parents will need to agree:

  • how much financial contributions will be
  • when they will be paid
  • how payments will be made

You could also agree to review or change your arrangement in the future.

Calculate child maintenance

You could use the child maintenance calculator to find out how much might be a fair amount, or use this arrangement tool to help you decide how you will share the support of your child. You will be able to share the results with the other parent.

Make your own arrangement

This discussion guide contains steps to take that may help you to have a conversation with the other parent to make a new arrangement.

If you need someone to help you to reach an agreement, ask a friend or family member, or find a local mediator to support you.

Many separated parents write down what they have agreed to, so they have something to refer to. [could be a contract?]

If you want to make your agreement legally binding, you can ask a court to formalise it with a consent order or minute of agreement. This means the agreement can be enforced through the courts if either parent doesn’t do what has been agreed.

Making an arrangement between yourselves might not be appropriate if you think the other parent poses a threat to you or your child, or if you are a victim of domestic abuse. If you feel making an arrangement could make you vulnerable, speak to one of our agents about your options. (-could link into statutory or telephone intervention)

If you cannot make your own arrangement

If you cannot make an arrangement between yourselves, either parent can ask the Child Maintenance Service to make a new, formal arrangement.
