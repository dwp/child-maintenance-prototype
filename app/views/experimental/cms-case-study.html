Leanna and Liams child maintenance service arrangement

Joan took care of her grandchildren, Sam and Billy, when her daughter Leanne split up with their father, Liam.

She asked both Leanne and Liam to give her some money every month to help with the costs of raising their children.

Leanne agreed to give Joan some cash every Sunday when she saw Joan and the kids for lunch. Liam ignored Joan’s request and wouldn’t answer her phone calls.

When she realised that Liam wasn’t going to help her with the costs of the children voluntarily, Joan paid £20 to ask the Child Maintenance Service to get involved.

The Child Maintenance Service tried to contact Liam, several times, but he ignored their communications. Because Liam wouldn’t speak to them, they used income information from HM Revenue and Customs to calculate how much he had to pay, which came out at £48 each week.

Liam received another letter from the Child Maintenance Service which told him he should be paying £48 direct to Joan every week. He didn’t want to pay so he ignored the letter. He also received a phone call from the Child Maintenance Service, and told them he wasn’t going to pay the amount that had been calculated.

Because Liam had told the Child Maintenance Service that he was not going to pay, Joan’s case was moved to the Collect and Pay scheme. He received another letter from the Child Maintenance Service which said they would now collect his payments from his wages through a deduction from earnings order (DEO). The money for child maintenance would then be passed on to Joan.

The letter told him that he would be charged for this service. He would now have to pay an extra 20%, so his new payments would be £57.80 each week, instead of £48, adding up to nearly £500 extra each year. It also stated that if the Child Maintenance Service needed to take other enforcement action he would have to pay for that as well, or they could take him to court.

The Child Maintenance Service also contacted Joan, to tell her the money would now be collected and passed on to her. However, she would have to pay a collection fee for this service, which would be 4% of Liam’s weekly payments, which added up to nearly £100 a year.

Joan was frustrated that this would mean less money for the children, but knew that it was the only way Liam would pay regularly. She needed the financial support so she could keep looking after the children.
