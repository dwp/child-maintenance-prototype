What is an arrangement?

A child maintenance arrangement is financial support towards your child’s everyday living costs when you’ve separated from the other parent. You and the other parent can arrange child maintenance yourself if you can agree.

There are many ways of making an arrangement that are not financial, for example, you could agree to share care of the child.

There are also many ways of making an arrangement that are financial.

For example, you could split the costs of specific items, or the non-resident parent could pay a proportion of their income to the parent with care.

The non-resident parent could also pay:

  • for things like housing costs, uniforms, clothes, school trips, or after school clubs
  • larger lump sums at certain times in the child’s life

What to include in an arrangement

Your arrangement can include whatever works for both parents.

You could agree to share care of your child, or school pick-up and drop-off responsibilities.

Make a financial arrangement

Many parents choose to make a financial arrangement. For example, the parent who does not live with the child might pay:

  • a fixed amount to the parent with care each month
  • for specific items for the child, like uniforms, school trips, or after school activities
  • for housing costs, like rent, mortgage payments, or household bills
  • larger lump sums at certain times in the child’s life

If you both agree to a financial arrangement, you will also need to agree how and when payments will be made, and if you will review your arrangement in the future.

You can use the child maintenance calculator (opens in new window or tab) to get an estimate of how much the Child Maintenance Service would calculate as a fair amount to pay.

How to pay child maintenance

There are several ways to pay child maintenance. The most suitable will depend on the arrangement you have and your circumstances.

Pay by standing order

If you’ve agreed on fixed regular payments, you could set up a standing order. This is where money is sent directly from one person’s bank or building society account to another person’s account.

A standing order helps both parents to plan and manage their finances, and keep a record of payments made.

Use a money transfer service

Another option is to use a money transfer service, like PayPal or MoneyGram. This method means you don’t have to share your bank details with the other parent.

Some of these services may charge a fee. Check with the service provider to make sure you understand if any charges will apply.

Other ways to pay

If you make your own arrangement, you can agree to change your payment method if your circumstances change.

For example, if the paying parent loses their job, both parents could agree to reduce the child maintenance payments until the paying parent is back in work.

Or, if the receiving parent needs to make urgent repairs to the home they live in with the children, the paying parent could help with the cost by paying a lump sum, instead of the regular payments.

If you need help to make an arrangement

There are many tools, guides and organisations that can help separated parents to reach an agreement for child maintenance.

This 'cost of raising your child' guide (opens in new window or tab) can help both parents think about the things children need. Use it to work out the true cost of bringing up your child, and how you can share that cost.

This arrangement tool (opens in new window or tab) has helped some parents to reach an agreement for child maintenance.

This discussion guide (opens in new window or tab) might help you to prepare for what can be a difficult conversation about arrangements for children.

Having a friend or family member there when you speak to each other can help separated parents to focus on the thing they’re trying to agree. They will need to stay calm and neutral, so choose someone you both trust.

If you don’t have a friend or family member who can stay neutral, find a local mediator (opens in new window or tab) to help you to have constructive conversations with the other parent.

Some parents find it useful to write down what they have agreed, so they have something to refer to in future.

Protecting yourself
Making an arrangement between yourselves might not be appropriate if you think the other parent poses a threat to you or your child, or if you are a victim of domestic abuse.

If making an arrangement could make you vulnerable, speak to one of our agents about your options.

Find out how to get help (opens in new window or tab) if you or someone you know is a victim of domestic abuse.

If your arrangement stops working

If you have an arrangement and it is no longer working, you could ask a friend, family member, or professional family mediator (opens in new window or tab) to help you get back on track.

A mediator can help you to:

  • work together to decide what is best for your child
  • reduce conflict between you
  • build trust in each other
  • improve communications
  • make a flexible, lasting agreement for child maintenance
If you cannot make your own arrangement

If both parents cannot agree, or something else is stopping you making an arrangement, the Child Maintenance service may be able to make and enforce an arrangement for you.

There is a £20 fee to apply.

The Child Maintenance Service is for when you and the other parent cannot agree your own arrangement.

If you cannot make an arrangement yourselves, you can apply to the Child Maintenance service.
